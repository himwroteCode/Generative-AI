Laptop Buying Guide

Learn the basics, then ask anything or get a config recommendation.

Learn the basics

CPU

The processor is the brain of your laptop. More cores help with multitasking and heavy apps; newer generations are faster and more efficient. For everyday use, a mid-range 4–6 core CPU (Intel i5/Ryzen 5 or similar) is usually enough. For coding, video editing, or gaming, consider 6–8 cores (i7/Ryzen 7).

Quick tip: Intel and AMD are both solid; compare by generation and core count, not brand alone.

RAM

RAM (memory) determines how many apps and browser tabs you can run smoothly. 8 GB is the minimum for light use. 16 GB is the sweet spot for most people—coding, many tabs, and light creative work. 32 GB is for heavy multitasking, virtual machines, or large media projects.

Quick tip: Prefer 16 GB if your budget allows; 8 GB can feel tight quickly.

Storage

Storage holds your OS, apps, and files. SSDs are much faster than HDDs—always choose SSD for the main drive. NVMe SSDs are faster than SATA SSDs. For size: 256 GB is tight; 512 GB is comfortable for most; 1 TB if you keep lots of media or games.

Quick tip: 512 GB NVMe SSD is a good default; upgrade if you work with large files.

Display

Screen size affects portability and comfort. 13–14" is light and portable; 15–16" is a balance of size and mobility; 17" is heavy but great for media. Resolution: 1080p (FHD) is standard and fine for most uses. 2K/4K looks sharper but uses more battery. Refresh rate: 60 Hz is standard; 90–120 Hz is nicer for smooth scrolling and gaming.

Quick tip: FHD (1920×1080) is the best value; higher resolution is optional.

GPU

Most laptops have integrated graphics (built into the CPU)—enough for office work, browsing, and light photo/video use. A dedicated GPU (NVIDIA/AMD) is needed for serious gaming, 3D work, or heavy video editing. If you don’t game or do professional creative work, integrated is usually enough.

Quick tip: Only pay for a dedicated GPU if you know you need it.

Ports & battery

USB-C and Thunderbolt (USB-C with more speed) are the modern standard; they can charge the laptop and connect monitors. Check how many USB (and USB-A) ports you need. Battery life varies a lot: smaller screens and efficient CPUs last longer; manufacturers’ numbers are often optimistic. Look for “all-day” claims (8+ hours) if you’re away from an outlet often.

Quick tip: At least one USB-C is useful for future-proofing; more is better if you use dongles.
